What is thyroid disease?

Thyroid disease refers to conditions where the thyroid gland doesn't produce the right amount of hormones. The thyroid, a butterfly-shaped gland located at the front of the neck, is part of the endocrine system and regulates vital body functions by releasing hormones like thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3). Its main role is to control metabolism through the process of converting food into energy. When the thyroid isn't functioning properly, it can affect the body's overall health and energy levels.

Thyroid Disease Types

Explore in-depth insights on various types of thyroid disorders. Understand the risk and symptoms for each condition, empowering yourself with the knowledge needed to protect your thyroid health and overall well being.

  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Thyroid Nodules
  • Goiter
  • Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

Hyperthyroidism

Hyperthyroidism happens when the thyroid gland produce too much thyroid hormone. This condition also is called overactive thyroid.

Who is at risk for hyperthyroidism?
You are at higher risk for hyperthyroidism if you:

  • Are a woman.
  • Are older than age 60.
  • Have been pregnant or had a baby within the past 6 months.
  • Have a family history of thyroid disease.
  • Have pernicious anemia, in which the body cannot make enough healthy red blood cells because it does not have enough vitamin B12.
  • Have type 1 diabetes or primary adrenal insufficiency, a hormonal disorder.
  • Get too much iodine, from eating large amounts of foods containing iodine or using iodine-containing medicines or supplements.

What are the symptoms of hyperthyroidism?
The symptoms of hyperthyroidism can vary from person to person and may include:

  • Nervousness or irritability.
  • Fatigue.
  • Muscle weakness.
  • Trouble tolerating heat.
  • Trouble sleeping.
  • Rapid and irregular heartbeat.
  • Weight loss.

Hypothyroidism

Hypothyroidism is a condition in which the thyroid gland does not produce enough thyroid hormones. Hypothyroidism is often called underactive thyroid.

Who is at risk for hypothyroidism?
You are at higher risk for hypothyroidism if you:

  • Are a woman.
  • Are older than age 60.
  • Have been pregnant or had a baby within the past 6 months.
  • Have a family history of thyroid disease.
  • Have autoimmune conditions like Hashimoto’s thyroiditis.
  • Had radiation treatment to your neck or upper chest.
  • Had thyroid surgery or a thyroid problem like goiter.
  • Have been treated with radioactive iodine or antithyroid medications.
  • Are deficient in iodine treatment.

What are the symptoms of hypothyroidism?
The symptoms of hypothyroidism can vary from person to person and may include:

  • Fatigue.
  • Weight gain.
  • Sensitivity to cold.
  • Muscle weakness.
  • Dry skin and hair.
  • Constipation.
  • Depression.
  • Slowed heart rate.
  • Hoarseness.

Thyroid Nodules

Thyroid nodules are lumps that form within the thyroid, which can be solid or fluid-filled. While most nodules are benign, some can be cancerous.

Who is at risk for thyroid nodules?
You are at higher risk for thyroid nodules if you:

  • Are a woman.
  • Are older than age 60.
  • Have a family history of thyroid nodules or thyroid cancer.
  • Have been exposed to radiation, especially in childhood.
  • Have iodine treatment deficiency.
  • Have a history of thyroid disorders, such as goiter.

What are the symptoms of thyroid nodules?
Many thyroid nodules don’t cause noticeable symptoms. However, symptoms may include:

  • A lump or swelling in the neck.
  • Difficulty swallowing or breathing.
  • Hoarseness or voice changes.
  • Throat pain.
  • Overproduction of thyroid hormones, which can lead to symptoms of hyperthyroidism.

Goiter

Goiter is an abnormal enlargement of the thyroid gland. While it is usually painless, a large goiter can cause discomfort and difficulty with breathing or swallowing.

Who is at risk for goiter?
You are at higher risk for goiter if you:

  • Are a woman.
  • Have iodine deficiency.
  • Are older than age 40.
  • Have a family history of goiter or thyroid disease.
  • Have autoimmune thyroid disease, such as Graves’ disease or Hashimoto’s thyroiditis.
  • Are pregnant or going through menopause, due to hormonal changes.
  • Have been exposed to radiation to the neck or chest.

What are the symptoms of goiter?
The symptoms of goiter can vary depending on the size and impact of the enlargement. They may include:

  • Visible swelling at the base of the neck.
  • Tightness in the throat.
  • Difficulty swallowing or breathing.
  • Hoarseness.
  • Frequently coughing.

Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

Hashimoto's thyroiditis is an autoimmune disorder in which the immune system attacks the thyroid gland, leading to inflammation and often causing hypothyroidism over time.

Who is at risk for Hashimoto's thyroiditis?
You are at higher risk for Hashimoto's thyroiditis if you:

  • Are a woman.
  • Have a family history of thyroid disease or autoimmune disorders.
  • Are middle-aged (although it can affect younger individuals as well).
  • Have another autoimmune disease, such as type 1 diabetes.
  • Have been exposed to radiation in the neck or upper chest.

What are the symptoms of Hashimoto's thyroiditis?
The symptoms of Hashimoto's thyroiditis often develop slowly over time and may include:

  • Fatigue.
  • Weight gain.
  • Sensitivity to cold.
  • Dry skin.
  • Hair loss.
  • Muscle weakness.
  • Depression.
  • Enlarged thyroid (goiter).

Thyroid Disease Factors

Explore key factors influencing thyroid health and disease risk. Learn how lifestyle, genetics, and environment impact thyroid function, and empower yourself to make better choices for thyroid health.

Hormonal Imbalances

High or low levels of thyroid hormones can be influenced by other hormonal fluctuations, particularly during pregnancy, menopause, or puberty, affecting overall thyroid health.

Smoking

Smoking affects thyroid function by increasing the risk of thyroid hormone imbalances and autoimmune thyroid diseases. It can also affect iodine absorption, which is crucial for thyroid health.

High Stress Levels

Chronic stress can negatively impact the thyroid by increasing cortisol levels, which may suppress thyroid function and contribute to hypothyroidism or other thyroid disorders.

Unhealthy Diet

A diet low in iodine, selenium, and other key nutrients can increase the risk of thyroid dysfunction. Foods high in goitrogens, such as soy or cruciferous vegetables in large amounts, can interfere with thyroid hormone production.

Physical Inactivity

Lack of regular exercise is associated with an increased risk of thyroid imbalances, contributing to weight gain and fatigue, common symptoms of hypothyroidism.

Family History and Genetics

A family history of thyroid disorders, such as autoimmune diseases like Hashimoto's thyroiditis or Graves' disease, increases the likelihood of developing similar thyroid conditions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Frequently asked questions about thyroid disease and related concerns.

What are the common types of thyroid diseases?

The most common thyroid diseases include hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid), hyperthyroidism (overactive thyroid), Hashimoto’s thyroiditis (autoimmune hypothyroidism), and Graves’ disease (autoimmune hyperthyroidism).

What are the symptoms of hyperthyroidism?

Symptoms of hyperthyroidism include weight loss, increased appetite, nervousness, irritability, rapid heartbeat, sweating, and difficulty sleeping. If you notice these symptoms, consult a doctor for medical check-ups.

How is hypothyroidism diagnosed?

Hypothyroidism is diagnosed through blood tests that measure levels of thyroid hormones (T3, T4) and thyroid-stimulating hormone (TSH). High TSH and low T4 levels often indicate hypothyroidism.

Can thyroid disease affect pregnancy?

Yes, thyroid problems can affect pregnancy. If not treated, hypothyroidism (an underactive thyroid) can cause issues like miscarriage, high blood pressure (preeclampsia), and early delivery. It's important to get proper treatment and regular check-ups during pregnancy to avoid complications.

Is thyroid cancer common?

Thyroid cancer is relatively rare compared to other cancers, but it is the most common cancer of the endocrine system. It is often treatable and has a high survival rate, especially if caught early.

What lifestyle changes can improve thyroid health?

To improve thyroid health, focus on maintaining a balanced diet rich in iodine, selenium, and zinc through foods like seaweed, dairy, nuts, and fish. Managing stress with practices such as meditation, yoga, or regular exercise is essential, as chronic stress can affect thyroid function. Avoid smoking, which can negatively impact thyroid health, and engage in regular physical activity to support metabolism and maintain overall health. Additionally, keep chronic conditions like diabetes, autoimmune diseases, and high blood pressure under control, as they can affect thyroid function. Finally, schedule regular check-ups with your healthcare provider to monitor thyroid health, particularly if you have a history of thyroid issues.
